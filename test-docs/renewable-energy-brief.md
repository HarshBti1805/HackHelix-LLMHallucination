# The State of Renewable Energy: 2024 Outlook

Global renewable energy capacity continued its rapid expansion through 2024, driven by falling costs and supportive policy frameworks. The International Energy Agency reported that renewable capacity additions reached 510 gigawatts in 2023, the largest single-year increase ever recorded, with solar photovoltaic installations accounting for approximately three-quarters of this growth.

According to research by Chen and Patel (2023) published in *Nature Energy*, the levelized cost of utility-scale solar has fallen by approximately 89% since 2010. This aligns with broader findings from the Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory, whose 2022 report estimated that wind power costs have dropped by roughly 70% over the same period.

Germany surpassed its 2030 renewable electricity target of 65% in late 2023, reaching 67.2% of grid consumption from renewable sources. China, meanwhile, installed more solar capacity in 2023 than the rest of the world combined, with total additions exceeding 200 gigawatts according to the National Energy Administration.

The United States Inflation Reduction Act, signed into law in 2021, has been credited with accelerating domestic clean energy investment. A Goldman Sachs analysis estimated the Act would mobilize $1.2 trillion in cumulative climate spending by 2032.

Battery storage deployment has also accelerated dramatically. BloombergNEF's 2024 Energy Storage Outlook projected global storage capacity would reach 1,400 gigawatt-hours by 2030, a fifteen-fold increase from 2023 levels. Lithium-ion battery pack prices fell to $139 per kilowatt-hour in 2023, the lowest on record.

Despite this progress, grid integration remains a significant challenge. A recent study by Ramírez and Okonkwo (2024) in the *Journal of Power Systems Engineering* found that 43% of interconnection queue capacity in major U.S. markets was held up by transmission constraints at the end of 2023.

Looking ahead, the IEA projects that renewables will account for over 90% of global electricity capacity expansion through 2028, with China alone expected to contribute nearly 60% of global additions during that period.
