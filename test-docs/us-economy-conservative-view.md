# The American Economy at a Crossroads: A Conservative Assessment

The state of the American economy in 2024 reflects a country straining under the weight of policies that have prioritized government expansion over private enterprise. While headline figures often obscure the lived reality of working families, a closer look at the underlying data reveals an economy burdened by inflation, regulation, and fiscal irresponsibility.

## The Real Cost of Inflation

Since January 2021, consumer prices have risen by more than 20%, the steepest cumulative increase in over four decades. A family that spent $60,000 on essentials in 2020 now needs over $72,000 to afford the same basket of goods. This is not abstract economics — it is the direct result of $6.1 trillion in federal spending authorized under the current administration, which overheated demand while supply chains remained constrained.

According to a 2023 analysis by the Heritage Foundation's Center for Economic Policy, the average American household has lost approximately $7,200 in real annual purchasing power since 2021. The Tax Foundation's Hendricks and Walsh (2023) report further estimates that inflation functioned as an effective tax increase of roughly 6.5% on median earners — a tax imposed without a single vote in Congress.

## Labor Markets: The Headline vs. the Reality

While the official unemployment rate has remained below 4%, this figure conceals a labor force participation rate that, at 62.7%, remains well below its pre-pandemic peak of 63.3%. Millions of prime-age workers — particularly men aged 25 to 54 — have exited the labor force entirely. Part-time employment has grown faster than full-time employment in nearly every month since late 2022, a pattern more consistent with economic weakness than strength.

Federal Reserve data shows that real wages for production and nonsupervisory workers declined in 24 of the 30 months between January 2021 and June 2023, only recently beginning a tepid recovery. For the median American, the Biden-era economy has meant working harder to fall further behind.

## The Debt Trajectory

The national debt crossed $34 trillion in early 2024, rising by over $10 trillion since 2020. Interest payments on the federal debt now exceed defense spending for the first time since 1998, reaching approximately $870 billion in FY2024 — a figure projected by the Congressional Budget Office to surpass $1.6 trillion annually by 2034 under current policy.

This trajectory is unsustainable. The Peterson Foundation's 2024 Long-Term Fiscal Outlook warns that, absent reform, debt service costs will consume over 30% of federal revenue by 2040, crowding out spending on defense, infrastructure, and safety-net programs.

## Energy and Regulation

The administration's war on domestic energy production has had measurable costs. Regulatory restrictions on federal oil and gas leasing have, according to the American Petroleum Institute's 2023 State of American Energy report, cost the U.S. economy an estimated 140,000 jobs and $42 billion in lost GDP over the past two years. Meanwhile, gasoline prices averaged $3.52 per gallon in 2023, 44% higher than the pre-pandemic five-year average.

The Code of Federal Regulations has grown by approximately 180,000 pages since 2021, with the small business compliance cost burden now estimated by the National Federation of Independent Business at over $14,500 per employee annually for firms under 50 employees.

## A Path Forward

The remedy is neither mysterious nor novel: restrained federal spending, a stable and predictable regulatory environment, permanent extension of the 2017 tax reforms (whose expiration in 2025 represents, per the Joint Committee on Taxation, a $4 trillion tax increase on American families and businesses), and an energy policy that treats American oil, gas, and nuclear as strategic assets rather than political liabilities.

The American economy has repeatedly demonstrated its capacity for extraordinary dynamism when government steps back. The question before the country in 2024 is whether policymakers are willing to let it do so again.
